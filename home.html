Three Modes, One Architecture

Referral Summarization

Faxed or scanned PDF referrals are processed into structured clinical summaries. Every extracted fact anchors to a specific location in the source document. Facts that cannot be anchored are suppressed.

Note Generation

From any referral summary, clinicians add their own notes and generate a complete clinical note. Every statement carries inline source citations—[1] [2] markers that trace back to the exact input that produced it. Citations strip automatically on clipboard copy for clean EMR paste.

LightScribe Note Expansion

Brief clinician-authored seed notes are expanded into conventional clinical structure. Every expanded sentence anchors to a span in the seed note. Content the clinician did not assert is suppressed—the same provenance architecture applied to a shorter source.

Why Provenance

Error Opacity, Solved

Most clinical AI produces fluent output that is wrong or incomplete in ways the clinician must catch by re-reading—generation opacity. ChartPrepper sidesteps this by solving error opacity: every fact traces to a source span, and unverifiable content is structurally suppressed. Audit shifts from "re-read everything" to "spot-check anchors."

Structural, Not Prompt-Based

Provenance is a structural property of the system, not prompt discipline. The model cannot emit content that fails the provenance check. Prompt engineering has a ceiling (Asgari et al., 2025)—this architecture operates below it.

Audited Accuracy

~0.64% error rate across 785 statements from 50 referrals, with zero fabricated facts. Published benchmarks for prompt-engineered approaches report 1–3% hallucination and 3.45% omission rates at ceiling.

The AI Scribe Problem

The Evidence

A VHA evaluation of 11 AI scribe tools (Reddy et al., Annals of Internal Medicine, 2026) found AI-generated notes scored lower than human notes across all 10 PDQI-9 quality domains—with the largest deficits in thorough, organized, and useful. The authors explicitly flag patient safety risk from key errors when these tools are adopted without rigorous oversight.

The Practitioner Experience

Physicians using commercial AI scribes report the same pattern: histories that are "too good," physical exam findings dropped despite being stated aloud, vitals omitted, and notes that require complete re-reading to verify. The time savings are real—but so is the re-read tax. As one physician put it: "it certainly requires going back and making sure the notes are accurate."

The Underlying Problem

AI scribes solve generation (audio → note) but leave the clinician to audit. The output is fluent and organized, but wrong or incomplete in ways that are invisible without line-by-line comparison to the encounter. This is error opacity—the same problem across modalities.

The Counter-Example

ChartPrepper is not competing with scribes—it is the counter-example to them. Where scribes generate unanchored prose the clinician must re-read, ChartPrepper provides structural provenance that makes audit a spot-check rather than a re-read. Its LightScribe mode inverts the scribe contract entirely: the clinician's seed note is the source of truth, so the model cannot lose what the clinician chose to assert. Every physician who has felt the "I have to re-read everything anyway" tax is primed to understand why provenance matters.
