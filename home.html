ChartPrepper

Unified provenance architecture for referral summarization and note expansion. Every fact in the output traces to a span in the source, or it is suppressed. This is a structural property of the system, not prompt discipline—the model cannot emit content that fails the provenance check.

  • Two modes, one architecture: referral summarization and LightScribe note expansion share the same provenance contract
  • Solves error opacity—errors become findable rather than requiring line-by-line re-reading
  • ~0.64% error rate across 785 statements from 50 referrals, with zero fabricated facts

The AI Scribe Problem

The Evidence

A VHA evaluation of 11 AI scribe tools (Reddy et al., Annals of Internal Medicine, 2026) found AI-generated notes scored lower than human notes across all 10 PDQI-9 quality domains—with the largest deficits in thorough, organized, and useful. The authors explicitly flag patient safety risk from key errors when these tools are adopted without rigorous oversight.

The Practitioner Experience

Physicians using commercial AI scribes report the same pattern: histories that are "too good," physical exam findings dropped despite being stated aloud, vitals omitted, and notes that require complete re-reading to verify. The time savings are real—but so is the re-read tax. As one physician put it: "it certainly requires going back and making sure the notes are accurate."

The Underlying Problem

AI scribes solve generation (audio → note) but leave the clinician to audit. The output is fluent and organized, but wrong or incomplete in ways that are invisible without line-by-line comparison to the encounter. This is error opacity—the same problem across modalities.

The Counter-Example

ChartPrepper is not competing with scribes—it is the counter-example to them. Where scribes generate unanchored prose the clinician must re-read, ChartPrepper provides structural provenance that makes audit a spot-check rather than a re-read. Every physician who has felt the "I have to re-read everything anyway" tax is primed to understand why provenance matters.
