Membrane Transport · One Battery, Many Drugs

The cell is a wall.
Sodium is the battery.

Every panel below is the same machine wearing different clothes. One ATP pump builds a sodium gradient; everything else spends it. Flip each cell between its native state and the drug that jams it, and watch the downstream effects fall out of the geometry — not out of memory.

The whole engine, in four lines

  1. The one pump: Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase sits basolateral, burns ATP, throws 3 Na⁺ out and pulls 2 K⁺ in. This is the only primary active transporter here.
  2. The consequence: intracellular Na⁺ stays low. So everywhere else, Na⁺ "wants" to fall back in. That downhill pull is stored energy — the battery.
  3. Two questions decode any other protein: (a) Na⁺-coupled? then Na⁺ rides in. (b) symporter or antiporter? sym → partner rides in with it; anti → partner gets thrown out.
  4. A drug usually jams one door. Block it, and the things that used to ride on Na⁺ pile up where they were — and that pile-up is the clinical effect.
Part I · Epithelia — cells with a front and a back

When the cell is a wall between two worlds

An epithelial cell separates a lumen (urine, gut, stomach) from blood. A tight junction belts it near the top, splitting its surface into two territories: apical (faces the lumen) and basolateral (faces blood). The pump lives on the blood side; the Na⁺-coupled entry door lives on the lumen side. That polarity is what lets the cell move things across itself in one direction.

1 SGLT2 Proximal tubule · kidney epithelial · polarized

Native physiology

Apical SGLT2 lets Na⁺ fall in from the urine and chains glucose to that fall — glucose is dragged in uphill. It then slips out the back to blood through GLUT2 (just flows down its gradient). Net: glucose reabsorbed, urine → blood.

  • ~90% of filtered glucose is recovered here
  • The Na⁺ that came in is bailed out the back by the ATPase

Door jammed · empagliflozin, dapagliflozin

Block apical SGLT2 and glucose can't be pulled out of the urine — it stays in the lumen and leaves in the urine.

  • Glucosuria → lowers blood glucose independent of insulin
  • Glucose in the lumen drags water → osmotic diuresis, mild volume/preload drop
  • Lost Na⁺ delivery downstream resets tubuloglomerular feedback → renoprotection
  • Sugary urine → genital mycotic infections; volume loss → euglycemic DKA risk
2 SGLT1 Enterocyte · small intestine epithelial · polarized

Native physiology · same machine as #1

Identical logic, different lumen. Apical SGLT1 couples Na⁺ from gut contents to glucose uptake; GLUT2 exits to blood. Where Na⁺ goes, water follows.

  • This is why glucose drives salt-and-water absorption

Why oral rehydration works · ORS

Cholera toxin floods the lumen with secreted fluid — but it leaves SGLT1 untouched. Put glucose and salt in the drink, and SGLT1 keeps pulling Na⁺ (and water) back in even mid-diarrhea.

  • Glucose + salt together → the coupled door reopens water absorption
  • A few cents of sugar/salt solution; one of the highest-impact ideas in medicine
  • Glucose alone or salt alone won't do it — the coupling is the point
3 NKCC2 Thick ascending limb · kidney epithelial · polarized

Native physiology

Apical NKCC2 hauls in Na⁺, K⁺, and 2 Cl⁻ together on the Na⁺ fall. K⁺ leaks back into the lumen via apical ROMK, leaving the lumen positive — and that positive charge pushes Ca²⁺/Mg²⁺ between the cells, back to blood.

  • The K⁺ recycling is what creates the voltage that drives Ca²⁺/Mg²⁺ reabsorption

Door jammed · furosemide, bumetanide

Block NKCC2 → lose Na⁺, K⁺, Cl⁻ into urine (powerful diuresis). But the lumen-positive voltage collapses too, so Ca²⁺/Mg²⁺ stop being reabsorbed.

  • Brisk natriuresis → strongest diuretics we have
  • Hypocalcemia + hypomagnesemia (voltage gone) — useful in hypercalcemia
  • More Na⁺ delivered downstream → hypokalemia
  • The same transporter sits in the inner ear → ototoxicity
4 NCC Distal convoluted tubule · kidney epithelial · polarized

Native physiology

Apical NCC brings Na⁺ and Cl⁻ in together. With less Na⁺ inside, a basolateral Na⁺/Ca²⁺ exchanger pulls Ca²⁺ out to blood — so Na⁺ handling and Ca²⁺ handling are linked here.

  • Fine-tuning segment — only ~5% of Na⁺, but it sets the final urine

Door jammed · hydrochlorothiazide, chlortalidone

Block NCC → modest natriuresis. Intracellular Na⁺ drops, which makes the basolateral exchanger pull in more Ca²⁺ from blood — so thiazides retain calcium (the opposite of loops).

  • Calcium retained → used for calcium kidney stones & osteoporosis
  • More Na⁺ delivered downstream → hypokalemia
  • Also hyponatremia, hyperuricemia, hyperglycemia (the classic thiazide quartet)
  • Loop wastes Ca²⁺, thiazide saves it — same logic, opposite segment
5 ENaC + Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase Collecting duct · principal cell epithelial · polarized

Native physiology · the K⁺ story

Apical ENaC (a pure channel, no coupling) lets Na⁺ in; the basolateral pump bails it to blood. Pulling positive Na⁺ out of the lumen leaves it negative, which sucks K⁺ out through apical channels into the urine. Aldosterone cranks up ENaC and the pump.

  • This is the site where K⁺ is lost — and why every upstream diuretic causes it

Door jammed · amiloride (ENaC) · spironolactone (aldosterone receptor)

Block ENaC (or the aldosterone signal upstream of it) → less Na⁺ reabsorbed, lumen stays less negative, so K⁺ stops leaving.

  • Potassium-sparing — the counterweight to loops/thiazides
  • Risk flips to hyperkalemia
  • Spironolactone also hits androgen receptors → gynecomastia (eplerenone avoids this)
6 H⁺/K⁺-ATPase Parietal cell · stomach epithelial · primary pump

Native physiology · a different ATPase

The exception that proves the rule: this apical pump burns ATP directly to push H⁺ into the stomach lumen and pull K⁺ in — no sodium battery involved. It's the final common step of acid secretion. The basolateral Cl⁻/HCO₃⁻ swap creates the post-meal "alkaline tide."

  • A primary active pump, like Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase — just moving protons

Door jammed · omeprazole, pantoprazole

PPIs covalently jam the proton pump itself. Because it's the last step, blocking it shuts acid down no matter what's signaling upstream (histamine, gastrin, vagus).

  • Profound acid suppression — heals ulcers, treats reflux
  • Less acid → worse absorption of B₁₂, iron, calcium, magnesium
  • Long-term: C. difficile risk, possible fracture/rebound hypersecretion
Part II · Non-epithelial cells — no front, no back

When there is no lumen at all

Here's the answer to your myocyte question: apical and basolateral mean nothing in a muscle cell. A myocyte isn't an epithelium — no tight junction, no lumen, no two-compartment wall. Its membrane faces interstitial fluid all the way around, and that fluid is in equilibrium with blood. So it's "all blood side," exactly as you guessed. The polarity that matters in a myocyte isn't spatial (top vs. bottom) — it's temporal (voltage rising and falling over time). The drugs act on that.

7 L-type Ca²⁺ channel Smooth muscle / cardiac myocyte non-epithelial · not a symporter

Native physiology · the correction

This is not a symporter and no sodium is involved. It's a plain voltage-gated channel: when the membrane depolarizes, it opens and Ca²⁺ simply falls in down its own gradient. That Ca²⁺ entry triggers contraction — vasoconstriction in vessels, force + pacemaker firing in the heart.

  • No apical/basolateral — just one membrane, opened by voltage
  • More open channels → more Ca²⁺ in → more contraction

Door jammed · amlodipine (vessels) · verapamil, diltiazem (heart too)

Block the channel → less Ca²⁺ enters → less contraction. Dihydropyridines (amlodipine) prefer vascular smooth muscle; non-DHPs also hit the heart.

  • Vessel relaxation → ↓ afterload, ↓ blood pressure
  • Reflex tachycardia & ankle edema with dihydropyridines
  • Verapamil/diltiazem slow rate & contractility → used for rate control
  • Now contrast this with panel 8 — calcium, but via the Na⁺ battery
8 Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase + NCX Cardiac myocyte non-epithelial · the antiporter you meant

Native physiology · "Ca²⁺ in or out depending"

This is the protein your intuition was reaching for. NCX is an antiporter: it normally lets 3 Na⁺ fall in and throws 1 Ca²⁺ out — riding the Na⁺ battery. Because it runs on that gradient, it can reverse if Na⁺ piles up inside. That reversibility is exactly the "in or out depending on what the body wants" you described.

  • The pump keeps Na⁺ low → NCX keeps throwing Ca²⁺ out → relaxation between beats

Door jammed · digoxin

Digoxin blocks the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase. Intracellular Na⁺ climbs → the Na⁺ battery weakens → NCX can no longer extrude Ca²⁺ (and may reverse) → Ca²⁺ builds up inside → stronger contraction.

  • Positive inotropy — the old heart-failure mechanism
  • Also boosts vagal tone → slows AV conduction (rate control in AF)
  • Narrow therapeutic window; hypokalemia worsens toxicity (K⁺ and digoxin compete at the pump)
  • Same calcium endpoint as a CCB — reached through the sodium battery, not a channel

One last frame. Panels 7 and 8 both end at "intracellular calcium," but they get there by opposite routes — a CCB shuts a channel, digoxin disables the pump that powers an exchanger. That's the payoff of the model: once you see whether a protein is a pump, a channel, a symporter, or an antiporter, the drug's whole side-effect profile is just the geometry running in reverse.
