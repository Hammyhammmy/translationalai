Three Geometries of Failure

Every clinical structure is one of three shapes — or a combination. Each shape fails in its own way, and each failure mode demands a different diagnostic question. Learn the three, and you have a triage move for every organ system you will ever see.

The whole thing in one paragraph

Layers stack. Failures are about depth — you go deeper until you find the defect. Conduits flow. Failures are about stations — you trace upstream from where the flow stalls. Networks branch. Failures are about territories — you map the distribution of the deficit and infer the branch point. Every anatomical structure is one of these three, or a combination of them. Get the geometry right and the diagnostic question writes itself.

1 — Layers

1
Layers
parallel depth · all present simultaneously
Which depth?

A layered structure is a stack of parallel planes, all present at the same time at the same location. The skin and the transversalis fascia exist together, separated by depth, not by route. A defect at one depth shows up as a symptom at the surface — but the symptom does not tell you the depth. The job is to localize how deep.

defect at depth · symptom at the surface · localize by going deeper
Anatomical examples
Abdominal wall (skin → fascia → muscle → transversalis → peritoneum). Skin and its appendages. Meninges (dura → arachnoid → pia). Cornea. Bowel wall histologic layers. Any structure where the diagnostic question is "how deep is the lesion."
Software parallel
The rendering stack. A request passes through every layer at once on its way out — HTML → DOM bindings → state store → cache → network → server. A bug at one layer manifests as wrong pixels at the surface. You localize by inspecting each depth in turn.

Diagnostic logic

Start at the symptom. Move one layer deeper. Ask: is the data correct at this depth? If yes, the lesion is shallower than you thought; if no, keep going. You stop at the first boundary where normal becomes abnormal — that is the lesion. Treatment happens at that depth, not at the surface where the symptom appeared.

Failure signature

A layer defect produces referred symptoms at the surface. Indirect hernia (defect at transversalis fascia) bulges through the skin. The patient points to skin; you fix the fascia.

2 — Conduits

2
Conduits
serial stations · flow moves through sequentially
Which station?

A conduit is a series of functional stations connected by directional flow. Production at one end, expulsion at the other. Every station does a job. When the flow stalls or the cargo is contaminated, the symptom appears at the exit — but the lesion is at the station where flow first failed. The job is to localize which station, by tracing upstream from where the flow visibly breaks.

flow stops at station 4 · pressure backs up to stations upstream · symptom shows at the exit
Anatomical examples
The urinary tract (production → collection → transport → storage → control → expulsion). The biliary tree. The GI tract. The airway. CSF flow. Anywhere with a defined source, sequential processing, and an exit.
Software parallel
Data processing pipelines, CI/CD, upload → transform → store. A failed deploy at the "test" stage halts everything downstream; everything upstream queues. You localize by walking the pipeline view from the failed stage upward — same move as tracing hydronephrosis up the ureter to the stone.

Diagnostic logic

Find where the flow visibly stops. Trace upstream. Every station above the blockage shows back-pressure damage (dilation, distention, parenchymal injury). Every station below shows under-perfusion (atrophy, no output, downstream dependents starved). The lesion is at the station where flow first fails — not at the symptomatic exit and not at the dilated stations above.

Failure signature

A conduit lesion produces upstream back-pressure and downstream absence. Renal stone at the UVJ (station 3): hydronephrosis above (stations 1-2 distended), empty ureter below, patient pointing at the flank where the dilated kidney capsule hurts.

3 — Networks

3
Networks
branching · distributed · multiple redundant paths
Which territory?

A network is a tree of branches descending from one or more sources, fanning out to a wide territory. A signal or supply travels along multiple paths simultaneously, and a defect at any branch starves only the territory it served — leaving the rest of the network functional. The deficit is the map. The job is to read which branch was lost by reading what's missing downstream.

one branch occluded · the deficit reveals the branch point · map the territory, find the lesion
Anatomical examples
Vasculature (arteries and veins). Lymphatics. The peripheral and central nervous system. Bronchial tree. Hepatic portal system. Anywhere a single source supplies a fan of dependent territories through branches.
Software parallel
Event systems, pub/sub, webhooks, auth propagation. An auth token touches middleware, route guards, WebSocket connections, multiple endpoints. A failure in token refresh doesn't stall at one station — it causes simultaneous failure across every downstream consumer. The diagnostic is "what's the territory served by this branch?"

Diagnostic logic

Read the deficit. The pattern of what is missing — which muscles are weak, which sensory dermatome is numb, which microservice stopped responding — defines the territory. The territory defines the branch. You localize by inversion: the network is intact except for one downstream area, so the lesion is at the last branch point upstream of that area.

Failure signature

A network lesion produces distributed simultaneous failure of a single territory, sparing everything else. Stroke of the MCA: contralateral face and arm weakness, expressive aphasia if left-sided. The territory is the diagnosis.

The Diagnostic Question Table

Pinned, because this is the thing to memorize. Every clinical encounter starts with naming the geometry of the structure involved — then the diagnostic question is determined.

Geometry The diagnostic question Find the lesion by Treatment principle
Layers "Which depth?" Going deeper until normal becomes abnormal. Treat at the depth of the defect — not at the surface where the symptom appears.
Conduits "Which station?" Tracing upstream from where flow visibly stops. Treat at the station where flow first failed — not at the upstream dilation or the silent exit.
Networks "Which territory?" Mapping what's missing; inferring the branch point upstream of it. Treat the branch — sometimes by reopening it, sometimes by accepting the territory and supporting collaterals.

Combinations

Pure geometries are rare. Most organs are combinations. Recognizing the mix is part of the skill.

  • Kidney — a conduit at the macro level (it sits at the head of the urinary tract), but internally a layered network: glomerulus → tubule → collecting duct (layers of filtration physiology) supplied by a branching vasculature. Renal infarct is a network problem; nephrotic syndrome is a layer problem; obstruction at the UPJ is a conduit problem. One organ, three geometries, three diagnostic moves.
  • Skin — primarily layers (epidermis / dermis / subcutaneous), but vascularized by a branching network and drained by lymphatics. Cellulitis is mostly layer; lymphangitis is a network read; necrotizing fasciitis is layers plus a network catastrophe inside them.
  • Lung — bronchial tree is a network of conduits ending in alveolar layers. Pneumonia is a layer problem at the alveolus; PE is a network problem at the pulmonary artery; mucus plugging is a conduit problem in a bronchus.
The interview question

When you don't know what to do, ask: "what geometry am I looking at, and what's the corresponding diagnostic question?" If you can answer that, the next test usually picks itself. If you can't, you don't understand the anatomy well enough to act yet — and the right move is to stop and figure that out, not to keep ordering things.
