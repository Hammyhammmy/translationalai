Common Consults: The ER & Inpatient Set

For the trainee taking first call. The ER sees the exit complaint. Your job is to localize the station where flow first failed — and to know which consults will not wait for the workup to complete.

The Consult Framework

Every urology consult arrives in tiers (a hierarchy of clinical reasoning, separate from the anatomical stations of the conduit). The tiers map cleanly onto how the call comes in:

TierWhat it isWho sees it
1 — Presenting complaint"Can't pee" · "Blood in urine" · "Pain" · "Fever"Triage nurse, ER attending
2 — Functional problemRetention. Obstructed flow. Loss of continence. Hemodynamic instability.ER physician
3 — Structural lesionStone at the UVJ. Prostatic enlargement. Clot in the bladder. Ischemic testis.You — on consult
4 — Root causeBPH. Anticoagulation. Bladder cancer. Cord torsion. Diabetes.Urology, downstream

The ER physician saw tier 1 and inferred tier 2. They called you because they need someone who can localize tier 3 — the station along the urinary conduit where flow first failed — and start planning around tier 4. That is the entire consult in one sentence.

What "first call" actually means

You are being trusted to do three things, in order. One, identify the consults that will not survive the workup — Fournier's, torsion, urosepsis with an obstructed system. Two, stabilize: decompress what's distended upstream, drain what's obstructed, hold pressure where it's bleeding. Three, localize the lesion well enough — to the conduit station that first failed — that the staff urologist can plan the operation by phone. The framework below exists so you do those three in the right order every time.

The Consults

Urgent
1. Urinary Retention
clock · hours (kidneys); minutes (pain)
Lesion station
Station 5 (prostate / urethra) or wiring (control)
obstructive vs neurogenic
Where it presents
Distended bladder, suprapubic pain
"can't pee" — Layer 1

Pattern

Two flavors. Obstructive retention: chronic outflow narrowing (BPH, stricture) decompensates acutely — usually triggered by alcohol, anticholinergics, decongestants, opioids, or postoperative state. Slow stream history. Big bladder, big PVR. Neurogenic retention: think cord — cauda equina, spinal cord injury, MS flare, diabetic autonomic neuropathy. Look for saddle anesthesia, leg weakness, bowel involvement, recent back pain. Acute on chronic is the most common ER picture: a man with years of LUTS who suddenly cannot void after a hip replacement and a few opioids.

Diagnostic move

Bladder scan first — anything over 300 mL with the complaint of inability to void is retention. Decompress with a Foley before you finish the workup. UA + BMP. Renal ultrasound if bilateral hydro is suspected or creatinine is up. Always ask the neuro questions out loud — saddle, legs, bowel, back pain. If any are positive, MRI lumbar spine STAT, not a urology consult.

Treatment

Foley decompresses Layer 2–3. Tamsulosin starts immediately if obstructive — improves voiding-trial success. Monitor for post-obstructive diuresis (urine output >200 mL/hr after decompression) — replace half of hourly output with IVF, recheck electrolytes. Trial of void in 3–7 days as outpatient if stable.

When to call urology

  • Can't pass the catheter — call before forcing. A false passage in a man with BPH or stricture costs you weeks.
  • Frank hematuria with retention — likely clot retention, needs 3-way irrigation, see below.
  • Retention with sepsis or AKI — admit, call now.
  • Any suspicion of cord pathology — neurosurgery first, urology second.
Urgent
2. Renal Colic / Ureteric Stone
clock · hours if infected; days if not
Lesion station
Station 2 — ureter (UPJ, pelvic brim, or UVJ)
three natural narrowings
Where it presents
Flank → groin → testis/labia
refers along the dermatome as the stone descends

Pattern

Sudden, severe, colicky pain. Patient cannot find a comfortable position — distinguishes from peritonitis, where they lie still. Microscopic hematuria common, gross hematuria possible. Nausea and vomiting prominent. Migration of pain toward the groin/scrotum suggests descent to the UVJ.

Diagnostic move

Non-contrast CT KUB is the test — sees stone, sees hydro, sees alternatives (AAA, appendicitis). UA looking for hematuria and infection. CBC + BMP. If pregnant: ultrasound first, then MRI if needed; CT is the last resort.

Treatment

  • Stones <5 mm in the distal ureter: ~80% pass. NSAIDs (ketorolac if renal function ok), antiemetic, tamsulosin (medical expulsive therapy — controversial but standard for distal stones >5 mm), strain urine, outpatient follow-up.
  • Stones 5–10 mm: variable. Most still pass with time. Outpatient urology in 1–2 weeks if uncomplicated.
  • Stones >10 mm: rarely pass spontaneously. Plan for intervention (ureteroscopy / SWL).

When to call urology

  • Infected obstructed system — fever + stone + hydronephrosis. This is urosepsis until proven otherwise. Drain immediately, see below.
  • Solitary kidney with obstruction.
  • Bilateral obstruction.
  • AKI from obstruction.
  • Intractable pain despite adequate analgesia.
  • Stone >10 mm — for elective planning, not in the ED.
Urgent
3. Gross Hematuria
clock · hours if clot retention; days if not
Lesion station
Any station from kidney to meatus
localization is the entire job
Where it presents
Color of urine — Layer 1
tells you nothing about depth

Pattern

Three patterns worth distinguishing by history. Initial hematuria (blood at the start, clearing) → urethral source. Terminal hematuria (clear, then blood at the end) → bladder neck / trigone. Total hematuria (blood throughout) → bladder or upper tract. Painless gross hematuria in an adult is malignancy until proven otherwise. Painful gross hematuria is usually stone or infection. Anticoagulation does not cause hematuria by itself — it unmasks an underlying lesion. Work it up the same way regardless.

Diagnostic move

UA + culture (rule out infection first; treat first if positive, then re-evaluate). CBC, BMP, coags. CT urogram for the upper tracts and bladder. Cystoscopy for the bladder mucosa — flexible, in clinic, the gold standard for bladder cancer detection. Assess clot retention at the bedside: palpate suprapubically, bladder scan if uncertain.

Treatment

If not retaining clot and stable: outpatient workup. If retaining clot: 3-way Foley (22–24 Fr, large bore) with continuous bladder irrigation (CBI). Manually evacuate clots with a Toomey before starting CBI — a CBI on top of a clot-filled bladder just inflates the system. Reverse anticoagulation if the bleeding is consequential. Type and screen, transfuse for symptomatic anemia.

When to call urology

  • Clot retention requiring CBI.
  • Failure to clear clots after manual evacuation and CBI.
  • Hemodynamic instability — bleeding from anywhere in the GU tract that drops a blood pressure is a now-call.
  • Recent urologic procedure with new bleeding — assume procedure-related, see post-op section.
  • Any gross hematuria needs outpatient urology follow-up. State this explicitly in the discharge plan; do not assume the patient will find their way to clinic.
Critical
4. Urosepsis
clock · hours — mortality climbs with each one
Lesion station
Stations 1–2 — obstructed, infected upper tract
closed space with bacteria
Where it presents
Fever, tachycardia, hypotension
systemic — Layer 1 in the worst way

Pattern

Fever + flank pain + hydronephrosis = obstructed pyelonephritis until proven otherwise. Diabetic, immunosuppressed, elderly, recently instrumented, or with a known stone are the at-risk groups. They can look surprisingly well one hour and crash the next. An infected, obstructed system is a closed-space infection. Antibiotics alone will not drain it. Pressure has to come off.

Diagnostic move

Lactate, blood cultures, UA + urine culture, CBC, BMP, coags. CT abdomen/pelvis with contrast (or non-contrast if AKI) to identify the obstruction. Sepsis bundle: fluids, broad-spectrum antibiotics, source control. Do not wait for cultures to start antibiotics — but understand that antibiotics are the second move, not the first.

Treatment

Drainage before antibiotics will work. Two options:

  1. Ureteral stent — urology, retrograde, in the OR. Faster if available.
  2. Percutaneous nephrostomy — IR, antegrade, at the bedside or in IR suite. Often the right call after hours.

The choice is local — whoever can drain faster. Both work. The wrong answer is "let's see if the antibiotics work."

When to call urology

Always, immediately, in parallel with calling IR. This is the consult where the phone call goes out before the CT result is back if the picture is clear enough. ICU admission expected.

Critical
5. Fournier's Gangrene
clock · time to OR is the only variable
Lesion station
Fascial planes of the perineum (layer geometry, not conduit)
necrotizing infection along Colles' / Buck's / dartos
Where it presents
Scrotal / perineal skin
often deceptively unimpressive early
If you suspect Fournier's, stop reading and call.

Pain out of proportion to exam findings in the perineum, scrotum, or groin — especially in a diabetic, obese, or immunosuppressed patient — is Fournier's until proven otherwise. Skin findings lag the fascial necrosis by hours. Crepitus is late. Hemodynamic instability is later. Time to operative debridement is the single variable that correlates with survival. Imaging delays the OR. Antibiotics do not penetrate dead tissue. Cut.

Pattern

Diabetic, immunosuppressed, alcoholic, perirectal abscess, recent perineal procedure, or chronic catheter. Severe pain in the perineum or scrotum. Skin can look like cellulitis early — pink, warm, swollen — then progress to dusky, purple, bullous, finally black. Crepitus is highly specific but not sensitive. Foul-smelling, "dishwater" purulent discharge if open. Systemic toxicity disproportionate to skin findings.

Diagnostic move

This is clinical. If you are sure, CT is a delay you cannot afford. If you are uncertain and the patient is hemodynamically stable, CT can confirm fascial gas and define extent — but a normal CT does not rule it out. LRINEC score is supportive, not diagnostic. Labs (CBC, BMP, lactate, CRP, glucose, coags) draw on the way to the OR.

Treatment

  • Broad-spectrum antibiotics with anaerobic coverage (e.g. pip-tazo + clindamycin + vancomycin, per local protocol). Start in the ED.
  • Aggressive resuscitation. ICU disposition.
  • Operative debridement. Surgical debridement is the treatment. Everything else is supportive. Expect repeat trips to the OR every 24–48 hours until clean.
  • Suprapubic catheter or diverting colostomy may be required depending on extent.

When to call urology

Now. Before any of the above. Call urology and general surgery together — the debridement crosses anatomic territories.

Critical
6. Priapism
clock · 4 h functional; 24 h is too late
Lesion station
Corpus cavernosum — ischemic outflow failure
or arterial fistula in non-ischemic
Where it presents
Painful, rigid penis >4 hours
glans typically soft, corpora rigid

Pattern

Two types, totally different problems. Ischemic (low-flow, veno-occlusive) is a compartment syndrome of the corpora. Painful, fully rigid, dark blood on aspiration. Causes: sickle cell, leukemia, intracavernosal injections, PDE5 inhibitors, trazodone, cocaine. Treat as an emergency. Non-ischemic (high-flow, arterial) follows perineal trauma. Partially rigid, painless, bright red blood on aspiration. Not an emergency — managed semi-electively with selective embolization.

Diagnostic move

History (sickle cell? trauma? drugs?). Cavernosal blood gas distinguishes — dark and acidotic = ischemic; bright and arterial = non-ischemic. Doppler ultrasound is confirmatory if uncertain.

Treatment — ischemic

  1. Aspirate the corpus cavernosum with a 19G needle through the glans or lateral shaft. Remove dark blood.
  2. Irrigate with saline.
  3. Inject phenylephrine — dilute to 100–500 mcg/mL, 1 mL aliquots every 3–5 minutes, monitoring BP and HR. Maximum dose ~1 mg total in most protocols.
  4. If >36 hours or refractory: surgical shunt. Permanent ED risk rises with every hour after the 4-hour mark.

Treatment — non-ischemic

Conservative initially. Many resolve. Selective pudendal artery embolization if persistent. Not a same-day procedure.

When to call urology

For any priapism >4 hours, call. If it's ischemic, you want urology hands-on for the aspiration and injection. If it's non-ischemic, you still want them planning the embolization conversation with IR.

Critical
7. Testicular Torsion
clock · 6 h to save the testis
Lesion station
Spermatic cord — vascular occlusion (network geometry — testicular artery)
testicular artery strangled by the twist
Where it presents
Acute scrotal pain
often radiating to the lower abdomen

Pattern

Adolescent or young adult, sudden severe scrotal pain, often with nausea and vomiting. Sometimes wakes from sleep. Testis high-riding, transverse lie, absent cremasteric reflex. Pain not relieved by elevation (Prehn's sign negative — differentiates from epididymitis where elevation helps, although do not stake the diagnosis on Prehn alone). Can present atypically in younger children and post-pubertal men — never bet against torsion in a sudden-onset acute scrotum.

Diagnostic move

Clinical. Doppler ultrasound shows decreased or absent testicular blood flow — but do not delay surgery for ultrasound if the clinical picture is convincing. The clock starts when the cord twists, not when the patient arrives.

Treatment

OR for emergent scrotal exploration, detorsion, and bilateral orchiopexy. Manual detorsion in the ED ("opening the book" — rotate the affected testis outward, away from midline) can be attempted while preparing for OR — relieves pain, may restore flow, does not replace the operation. Bilateral orchiopexy because the contralateral side has the same anatomic predisposition (bell-clapper deformity).

When to call urology

Immediately upon suspicion. NPO, mark the patient for OR. Salvage rate is >90% at <6 hours, <10% at >24 hours. The consult is not "rule out torsion" — it's "this is torsion until urology rules it out in the OR."

Urgent
8. Traumatic GU Injury
clock · mechanism-dependent
Lesion station
Stations 1–5 — any conduit station, mechanism-dependent
force vector predicts depth
Where it presents
Hematuria, perineal bruising, blood at meatus
small surface signs, big underlying injury

Renal trauma

Blunt — usually MVC, sports impact, fall. Penetrating — gunshot or stab. Hematuria does not correlate with severity; a major renal pedicle injury can have minimal hematuria. CT abdomen/pelvis with IV contrast (delayed phase for collecting system) grades the injury (AAST I–V). Most blunt low-grade injuries (I–III) managed non-operatively. Active extravasation or hemodynamic instability → angio-embolization or OR. Watch for delayed bleeding from a pseudoaneurysm — readmit threshold low.

Bladder trauma

Pelvic fracture + gross hematuria = bladder injury until proven otherwise. CT cystogram (retrograde fill with contrast, then image) is the test — not a contrasted CT alone, which underdiagnoses by 50%. Two patterns: extraperitoneal (most common, with pelvic fracture) — managed with Foley drainage 10–14 days, repeat cystogram before catheter removal. Intraperitoneal (blunt trauma to a full bladder, classically a seatbelt injury) — surgical repair. Always.

Urethral trauma

Blood at the meatus is a stop sign. Do not place a Foley until urethral injury is ruled out. Two patterns: posterior (pelvic fracture, "high-riding prostate" on DRE — though that finding is unreliable) → retrograde urethrogram (RUG); often managed with suprapubic catheter and delayed repair. Anterior (straddle injury) → RUG; may be primarily repaired or stented depending on the lesion.

When to call urology

  • Any blood at the meatus before catheterization.
  • Gross hematuria in a trauma patient.
  • Pelvic fracture with any GU sign.
  • Penetrating GU trauma — always operative until proven otherwise.
  • Renal injury AAST grade III or higher.
Urgent
9. Post-Operative Complications
clock · variable; treat the new finding seriously
Lesion station
Whatever station was operated on
prostate, kidney, bladder, ureter
Where it presents
Drain output, urine color, fever, ileus
all of which can mean several things

Hematuria post-TURP

Some bleeding is expected — pink urine, mild clots, clearing over 24–48 hours. Bright red, brisk, or with clots that overwhelm the CBI is not expected. Increase irrigation rate, manually evacuate clots, check coags, check that the catheter balloon isn't pulled into the prostatic fossa applying tamponade incorrectly. Persistent bleeding → return to OR for fulguration.

Urine leak post-partial nephrectomy / post-pyeloplasty

High drain output that smells like urine, or drain creatinine markedly higher than serum creatinine, confirms urine leak. Most heal with prolonged drainage and a ureteral stent to lower the pressure in the collecting system (decompress upstream so the leak closes downstream). Persistent leak → percutaneous nephrostomy + stent. Reoperation rare.

Catheter pulled / fell out post-prostatectomy or post-urethroplasty

Do not blindly replace. Call urology. A urethral catheter passing through a fresh anastomosis can disrupt it. Often requires cystoscopic placement over a wire. Treat this as a same-night call.

Anuria post-cystectomy with ileal conduit

Stoma not draining. First — is the stoma viable (pink)? Dusky stoma is a vascular emergency. Then — check the catheters in the conduit; mucus plug is the usual cause. Irrigate the conduit catheters gently. If still no output, imaging of the ureteroenteric anastomosis.

Ileus + fever + leukocytosis post-pelvic urologic surgery

Think urinoma, abscess, ureteric injury. CT abdomen/pelvis with delayed images. The clinical picture you do not want is "anastomotic leak masquerading as ileus."

When to call urology

Any unexpected finding in the first 30 days after a urologic operation belongs on the operating surgeon's phone, not just on a generic urology consult. The surgeon knows what their reconstruction looks like and what should and should not be happening.

The Triage Matrix

The principle: the rate of layer failure determines tempo. If the lesion advances faster than your diagnostic window, you act first and confirm later. If it advances slower, you image and plan.

The framing question

For every consult, ask: what is the rate of layer failure, and does it outpace my diagnostic window? If yes → act now, confirm later. If no → confirm, then act. This is the one habit that separates a useful first-call from one that holds the operation up.

When to Wake Up the Urologist

The PA needs rules, not judgment. Use these. They are intentionally over-inclusive on the side of waking someone up.

Wake them up. Right now.
  • Suspected Fournier's gangrene. No imaging required.
  • Suspected testicular torsion within the operative window.
  • Obstructed pyelonephritis — fever + hydronephrosis + sepsis physiology.
  • Ischemic priapism >4 hours.
  • GU bleeding with hemodynamic instability.
  • Penetrating GU trauma.
  • Failed catheterization in acute retention with rising creatinine or sepsis.
  • Any post-operative urology patient within 30 days with a new acute finding.
Call within the shift, not at 0300.
  • Successful Foley placement for retention — patient stable, no AKI. Sign-out call is fine.
  • Renal colic, controlled pain, no fever, no AKI, stone passable.
  • Gross hematuria, no clot retention, stable hemoglobin, anticoagulation reviewed.
  • Stable Grade I–II renal trauma.
  • Uncomplicated UTI in a normal host.
Handle yourself; pass to clinic.
  • Asymptomatic microscopic hematuria with a clean UA and no risk factors — workup as outpatient.
  • Stable chronic retention without overflow incontinence or AKI.
  • Phimosis without obstruction.
  • Non-painful scrotal masses (with documentation and follow-up arranged).
The default

When in doubt, call. The cost of an unnecessary call is one annoyed urologist. The cost of a missed Fournier's or torsion is a patient who loses tissue or dies. The asymmetry is not subtle. You will lose nothing in the long run by being the PA who called at 0300 about a possible torsion that turned out to be epididymitis. You will lose a lot by being the one who didn't.

A Worked Consult

POD1 after right total hip replacement. 68-year-old man. Has not voided since the Foley came out 6 hours ago. ER is involved because the floor team is uncertain. Call comes to you.

1
What the ER / floor sees · tier 1
"Post-op patient can't void."
Floor team has the surface complaint and a generic post-op picture. No vitals abnormality yet. They want to know if they can replace the catheter, or whether something else is going on. The floor's instinct is to put the Foley back. Your instinct should be to localize before you instrument.
2
What you assess first · tier 2
Bladder scan. Vitals. Med list.
Scan: 720 mL. Confirms retention — the symptom is real and the bladder is the reservoir, not just a sensation issue. Vitals: HR 88, BP 138/82, afebrile. No sepsis. Medications since OR: spinal anesthetic, fentanyl PCA, ketorolac, ondansetron, oxybutynin (someone wrote it for "bladder spasms" pre-op and it never came off). The PCA and oxybutynin are the immediate functional cause. Underneath them may sit chronic BPH.
Working frame: drug-precipitated acute retention, almost certainly on a background of LUTS.
3
Targeted history · tier 3 (structural lesion)
"Have you had trouble peeing before this?"
Yes. Slow stream for two years. Nocturia twice a night. Has been "meaning to see somebody about my prostate." Never had retention before. No back pain, no leg weakness, no saddle anesthesia, no bowel change. No prior catheterization, no instrumentation. This is acute-on-chronic outflow obstruction precipitated by anticholinergic + opioid load + supine recovery, with a Layer 5 substrate (prostate).
Off the list: cord pathology. On the list: BPH unmasked.
4
Decompress · relieve back-pressure
Foley, 16 Fr, sterile, single attempt.
Passes without difficulty. 780 mL out over the first 15 minutes. No gross hematuria. Patient instantly comfortable. You are now in the diagnostic window the catheter bought you — do not waste it. Order: BMP, UA, urine culture (it'll grow eventually if anything; better to know now). Confirm afebrile, watch for the next hour.
Result: creatinine 1.1 (baseline 1.0). UA clean. No infection. No AKI worth a consult on its own.
5
What urology needs to know · tier 4 (root cause)
The 30-second handoff.
When you call the staff urologist or the resident, give them this, in this order:
  1. "68 y/o man, POD1 right THA, acute retention 720 mL on scan."
  2. "Two-year history of LUTS, never retained before. No neuro symptoms."
  3. "Foley passed easily, drained 780 mL, no hematuria."
  4. "BMP normal, UA clean, afebrile."
  5. "On PCA and oxybutynin — I'd like to stop the oxybutynin and start tamsulosin."
  6. "Plan: leave Foley for 48–72 hours, trial of void after offending meds are off, urology clinic follow-up."
That is the entire consult. You have localized, decompressed, named the root cause, and offered a plan. The urologist either agrees and goes back to sleep, or modifies the plan by one variable. Either way you have done your job.
6
Management · station 5 (urethral outlet)
Foley + tamsulosin + stop the offenders.
Discontinue oxybutynin. De-escalate PCA to PRN oral opioid plus scheduled acetaminophen. Start tamsulosin 0.4 mg nightly. Foley to leg bag, training the patient and family on care. Document: "Acute urinary retention post-arthroplasty, multifactorial — anticholinergic, opioid, supine state — on a background of clinical BPH. Decompressed. TOV planned in 48–72 hours after offending agents discontinued. Outpatient urology referral placed."
7
The disposition hinge · what you are watching for
The TOV decides everything.
Trial of void in 48–72 hours. Three outcomes, three dispositions:
  • Voids well, low PVR (<150 mL) → home on tamsulosin, outpatient urology in 4–6 weeks. Discharged when ortho clears him.
  • Voids partially, PVR 200–400 mL → judgment. Often replace catheter, try again in a week as outpatient. Tamsulosin continued.
  • Fails to void, or PVR >400 mL → Foley with leg bag home, outpatient urology in 1–2 weeks, frank conversation about BPH workup and likely intervention (medical vs surgical).

The hospital stay is no longer about urology — orthopedics owns the disposition for the hip. Urology's contribution is the bladder plan that travels home with him.

What this consult teaches

You did not need urodynamics. You did not need cystoscopy. You did not need to wake anyone up. You localized the lesion (Layer 5, prostate), identified the precipitant (Layer 4, drug-induced), decompressed the functional problem (Layer 2, retention), and handed off a plan that survives discharge. Most of your consults will look like this. The point of all the other entries in this document is so that you recognize the ones that don't — and call accordingly.
