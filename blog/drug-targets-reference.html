Mechanisms of Action · Organized by Target Type

Five frames.
Every drug is in one of them.

A drug's whole behavior — what it does, what it doesn't, its side effects — falls out of what kind of thing its target is. Sort the target into one of five frames and the rest follows. The first two frames are interactive: flip each between native physiology and the drug that acts on it. URO marks urology-relevant targets.

Frame 1

Membrane ion movement

The target is a protein that moves or gates an ion across a membrane: a pump, a channel, or a coupled transporter running on the sodium battery. Almost everything here is the same machine — one ATP pump builds the Na⁺ gradient; the rest spends it. A drug jams one door, and whatever used to ride through it piles up on one side. That pile-up is the clinical effect.

Frame 2

Receptors → second messengers

No ion crosses anything. A signal lands on a surface receptor (usually a GPCR), which switches on an internal messenger — cAMP, cGMP, or Ca²⁺ via IP₃. The messenger does the work, and an enzyme later destroys it. Drugs act anywhere on that chain: block the receptor, or block the enzyme that degrades the messenger so it piles up. This is where erections, the bladder, and the prostate live.

Frame 3

Enzyme inhibition

The simplest frame. The target is a catalyst that turns substrate into product. Block it and the product falls (or the substrate backs up). No membrane, no messenger — just a reaction switched off.

Frame 4

Intracellular & nuclear receptors

The target sits inside the cell. A lipophilic hormone diffuses through the membrane, binds a receptor that then changes which genes are transcribed. Effects are slow (hours to days) because they run through protein synthesis. Filing enzalutamide under "enzyme inhibitor" would be the same category error as calling a calcium channel a symporter — so it gets its own frame.

Frame 5

Large molecules & DNA-level agents

Targets too big or too fundamental for the frames above: extracellular proteins neutralized by antibodies, and the DNA/cytoskeleton machinery hit by classical chemotherapy. Kept brief — the point is to see where they sit relative to everything else.

How to use this. When you meet a new drug, don't memorize it — ask what its target is. Pump, channel, or transporter → Frame 1, and its side effects are the ion pile-up. GPCR or PDE → Frame 2, and the effect is a messenger going up or down. Catalyst → Frame 3. Intracellular receptor → Frame 4. Antibody or DNA-level → Frame 5. The frame predicts the behavior.

A teaching reference, deliberately schematic — stoichiometry, subtypes, and segment detail are simplified for clarity, and a few mechanisms (SGLT2 cardioprotection, exact NCX directionality under digoxin, DCT calcium handling) remain genuinely debated. Not a prescribing resource.
