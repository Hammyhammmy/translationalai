Ureteric (JJ) stent
internal · bypasses the ureter
Where it sits

A soft tube with a coil (J) curled in the kidney and one in the bladder, lying the length of the ureter — it keeps urine draining past a blockage from the inside, with nothing on the outside.

Why it's placed

An obstructing stone, a stricture, tumour compression, or to protect a repair/anastomosis.

What goes wrong
  • Stent symptoms — frequency, urgency, flank pain on voiding (reflux up the stent)
  • Encrustation / blockage and infection if left too long
  • Forgotten stent — must be tracked and removed/changed
In plain words
What it is
A thin internal tube holding the drainage tube from the kidney open. It is entirely inside — there is no bag.
What to watch for
Some urgency and a little blood are common; fever, severe pain, or being unable to pass urine need review. It must be removed or swapped on time.

Nephrostomy tube
external · diverts above the blockage
Where it sits

A tube placed through the back directly into the kidney's collecting system, draining urine to an external bag — it diverts above an obstruction the ureter can't be passed from below.

Why it's placed

An obstructed, infected kidney (emergency decompression) or when a stent cannot be passed retrograde.

What goes wrong
  • Dislodgement / falling out (urgent — the track closes)
  • Blockage, kinking, or leakage around the site
  • Site infection
In plain words
What it is
A tube in the back that drains the kidney into a bag, used when urine cannot get out the normal way.
What to watch for
If it falls out, stops draining, or there is fever, seek help promptly — the channel can close within hours.
